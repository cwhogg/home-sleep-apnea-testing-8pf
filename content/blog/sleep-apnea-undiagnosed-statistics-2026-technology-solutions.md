## Why Sleep Apnea Goes Undiagnosed: The Perfect Storm of Barriers

## Can You Get a False Positive on a Sleep Apnea Test?

Both wearable devices and home sleep tests can produce false positives, but understanding the causes helps interpret results appropriately:

### Wearable Device False Positives
- **Movement during sleep** can mimic breathing interruptions
- **Cardiac arrhythmias** may trigger apnea detection algorithms
- **Medication effects** (particularly sedatives) can alter normal patterns
- **Sleep environment changes** (travel, stress) may affect baseline readings

### Home Sleep Test False Positives
- **Sensor displacement** during sleep can create artifact readings
- **Central sleep apnea** may be misclassified as obstructive
- **Periodic limb movements** can trigger arousal detection
- **Technical malfunctions** occasionally produce invalid data

**Minimizing False Positives:**
- Use **multiple night testing** to establish consistent patterns
- **Clinical correlation** with symptoms and risk factors
- **Professional interpretation** by sleep medicine physicians
- **Confirmatory testing** when results don't match clinical picture

## What Gets Misdiagnosed as Sleep Apnea?

Several conditions can mimic sleep apnea symptoms or produce similar test findings:

### Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome (UARS)
- **Partial airway obstruction** without complete apneas
- **Arousal-based symptoms** similar to OSA
- **Standard testing** may miss subtle breathing effort increases
- **Treatment approach** often identical to mild OSA

### Central Sleep Apnea
- **Brain signal dysfunction** rather than physical obstruction
- **Different treatment requirements** (often BiPAP vs. CPAP)
- **Associated conditions** include heart failure and neurological disorders
- **Home testing** may misclassify as obstructive sleep apnea

### Nocturnal Hypoventilation
- **Shallow breathing** without discrete apnea events
- **CO2 retention** during sleep
- **Common in obesity** and neuromuscular conditions
- **Requires different therapeutic approach**

### Sleep-Related Movement Disorders
- **Restless leg syndrome** can fragment sleep similarly
- **Periodic limb movements** cause arousals and fatigue
- **May coexist** with sleep apnea, complicating diagnosis
